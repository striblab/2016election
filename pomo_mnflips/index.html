Crumbling the blue wall
To secure his stunning presidential upset, Donald Trump managed to flip five battleground states that Barack Obama had won in 2008 and 2012 in defiance of polls and statistical forecasts. Hillary Clinton could not rely on the "blue wall" Democratic candidates had relied upon for the last several presidential elections as states with big electoral values fell by razor-thin margins to the Republican ticket.
County-level domination
On a more granular county-level analysis of 2016's presidential vote, Trump continued the Republican trend of dominating areas outside major metropolitan cities, not just in key battlegrounds, but across the country.
Tilting Minnesota
This trend was felt across Minnesota too, as only nine counties in a state that has gone Democratic in all but three elections since 1932 leaned toward Clinton. Heavy Democratic voting in Hennepin and Ramsey counties was the primary force that barely kept Minnesota in the blue column. Clinton only carried the state by about 42,000 votes, the slimest margin of any Democrat to claim Minnesota's electoral votes.
Outstate domination
Trump won all but five of Minnesota's outstate counties and flipped 19 that Obama carried in 2012, whereas Clinton didn't lead any that Mitt Romney took that election. Every county Trump took from the Democrats was outside the seven county metro area.
Nationally high turnout
Minnesota once again ranked the highest among voter turnout for the entire country, with 74 percent of the state's voters casting ballots, comparable to 2012's 76 percent turnout and 20 points higher than a national turnout that dropped from years previous. Note: All turnout data is preliminary and may not reflect provisional ballots, absentee voters or final vote counts.
Race and education
The county-level vote in Minnesota cut across racial and and education lines, with Trump leading areas with lower minority populations and fewer people with college degrees.
A major shift
Minnesota counties used to be more Democratic than Republican, with rural counties being more evenly distributed between the parties. But starting in 2000, outstate Minnesota has turned redder as Democratic bastions have gradually been confined to the Iron Range and metro area.
Poll crash
A vast majority of the polls and forecasts got the election wrong, both nationwide and in Minnesota. While Clinton's polling percentages were much closer to the mark, pollsters significantly underestimated Trump's support in Minnesota, sometimes by more than 20 points. The Star Tribune's final Minnesota Poll, for instance, correctly placed Clinton at 47 percent, but only had Trump at 39 percent, six points behind his actual finish on election night.
Swing state status
Minnesota, as determined by the presidential result averaging Cook Partisan Voting Index (PVI), has long been more Democratic across presidential election than the country's average. But that's been changing over the years, and 2016 may have finally pushed the state out of the Democratic blue wall and into the realm of swing states. While Cook hasn't yet released its ratings following this year's election, a Star Tribune estimate of comparisons between the state and national two-party vote averages from the last two elections gives Minnesota a new PVI of D+1.
Turning Obama counties
Across the country, Trump flipped 221 counties that had gone for Obama in 2012, while Clinton only managed to turn 22 that had gone for Romney. Many of Trump's pickups were in the Rustbelt and Midwest in states where he carried the biggest upsets.
