Crumbling the blue wall
To secure his stunning presidential upset, Donald Trump managed, in defiance of polls and statistical forecasts, to flip six battleground states that Barack Obama had won in 2008 and 2012. Hillary Clinton could not rely on the "blue wall" Democratic candidates had relied upon for the past several presidential elections, as states like Michigan and Pennsylvania with big electoral values fell by razor-thin margins to the Republican ticket.
County-level domination
On a more granular county-level analysis of 2016's presidential vote, Trump continued the Republican trend of dominating areas outside major metropolitan cities, not just in key battlegrounds, but across the country.
Tilting Minnesota
This trend was felt across Minnesota too, as only nine counties in a state that has gone Democratic in all but three elections since 1932 leaned toward Clinton. Heavy Democratic voting in Hennepin and Ramsey counties was the primary force that barely kept Minnesota in the blue column. Clinton carried the state by only about 43,000 votes, the slimmest margin of any Democrat to claim Minnesota's electoral votes since John F. Kennedy in 1960.
Outstate domination
Trump won all but five of Minnesota's outstate counties and flipped 19 that Obama carried in 2012, while Clinton didn't win any that Mitt Romney took. Every county Trump took from the Democrats was outside the seven-county metro area.
Isolating the cities
Across outstate Minnesota, most precincts voted for Trump by large margins, often by 10 percentage points or far more, isolating Minneapolis, St. Paul and their first-tier suburbs as the main centers of significant DFL voting.
Urban DFL bastions
The vast majority of Clinton's votes in Minnesota came from Minneapolis, St. Paul and their closest suburbs.
Nationally high turnout
Minnesota once again ranked the highest in the entire country in voter turnout, with 74 percent of the state's voters casting ballots, comparable to 2012's 76 percent turnout and 20 points higher than a national turnout that dropped from previous years. Note: All turnout data, obtained Nov. 10th, is preliminary and may not reflect provisional ballots, absentee voters or final vote counts.
Divided voters
There were clear divisions in Minnesota’s voting patterns across racial and education lines. Counties where Trump won have populations that are, on average, about 90 percent white non-Hispanic, while counties Clinton won average 75 percent white. Education was an even sharper divide, with counties that supported Clinton averaging 42 percent with a bachelor’s degree or higher and Trump-supporting counties averaging 24 percent.
A major shift
Minnesota counties used to be more Democratic than Republican, with rural counties being more evenly distributed between the parties. But starting in 2000, outstate Minnesota has turned redder as Democratic bastions have gradually been confined to the Iron Range and metro area.
Poll crash
A vast majority of the polls and forecasts got the election wrong, both nationwide and in Minnesota. While Clinton's polling percentages were much closer to the mark, pollsters significantly underestimated Trump's support in Minnesota, sometimes by more than 20 points. The Star Tribune's final Minnesota Poll on Oct. 25th, for instance, placed Clinton just one point ahead of her 46 percent finish, but only had Trump at 39 percent, six points behind his actual result.
A new swing state?
Minnesota, as determined by the Cook Partisan Voting Index (PVI), which averages presidential results, has long been more Democratic in presidential elections than the country's average. But that's been changing over the years, and 2016 may have finally pushed the state out of the Democratic blue wall and into the realm of swing states. While Cook hasn't yet released its ratings after this year's election, a Star Tribune estimate of comparisons between the state and national two-party vote averages from the past two elections gives Minnesota a new PVI of D+1.
Turning Obama counties
Across the country, Trump flipped 229 counties that had gone for Obama in 2012, while Clinton managed to turn only 21 that had gone for Romney. Many of Trump's pickups were in the Rust Belt and Midwest, in states where he scored his biggest upsets.
Shifting red
Not only did a significant number of counties flip from being Democratic to Republican, 605 counties -- about 20 percent of all counties in the nation -- voted for Trump at higher rates than they did Romney in 2016, signifying high Republican turnout across the country.
